Nicholas Tomlin

I'm a first-year doctoral student at Berkeley AI Research, currently interested in computational linguistics, reinforcement learning, and neuroscience. Recently, I've been thinking about:

  • Can we leverage structured world knowledge to improve the quality of natural language understanding? Can we build reinforcement learning systems which produce this structured world knowledge?
  • How can we measure the degree to which certain models encode linguistic information? Generally, what does it mean for information to be encoded in a representation?

In general, I'm interested in NLP methods that perform well on small datasets. Throughout my work, I hope to build models and systems which are successful on low-resource languages.


Past Research

Emergent Communication and Reinforcement Learning

  • Studied how agents may cooperate to develop a "language" in certain multi-agent reinforcement learning environments, and how to induce more natural linguistic structure via information theoretic concepts such as noisy-context surprisal.
  • Presented at 2018 Neural Information Processing Systems Workshop on Emergent Communication. [Abstract] [Paper]

Pragmatic Dialogue Agents for Negotiation

  • Studied how the rational speech acts (RSA) model may be used to achieve goal-oriented dialogue in neural conversational agents. Built pragmatic agents for a negotiation task. [Code]
  • Work done at Stanford CSLI under NSF REU (award #1659585).
